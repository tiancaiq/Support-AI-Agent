# Robot Vacuum And Vacuum-Mop Buying Guide

## Core Buying Criteria
1. Start with your home scenario: small apartment, pet home, large home, hardwood, carpet, or mixed floors.
2. For basic homes, choose at least 3000 Pa suction; for carpet-heavy homes, choose 4000 Pa or higher.
3. Laser navigation is usually more accurate in complex layouts; visual navigation can be cost-effective.
4. 3D structured-light obstacle avoidance is stronger for small objects than basic infrared avoidance.
5. Small homes under 80 square meters usually need at least 60 minutes of runtime.
6. Large homes over 120 square meters should target 120+ minutes and resume-after-charge.
7. Choose a clean-water tank of 300 ml or more for larger homes.
8. Rotating mop pads usually clean stains better than passive mop pads.
9. Mop lift is important for hardwood and carpet boundaries.
10. Auto-empty is useful for busy users and pet homes.
11. Dust bags of 2.5 L or larger reduce replacement frequency.
12. Hot-air drying helps prevent mop odor and mildew.
13. Leading brands often provide better quality control and after-sales service.
14. Check local repair availability for smaller brands.
15. Measure furniture clearance before buying; low beds may require a slim robot.

## Navigation And Avoidance
16. SLAM-based mapping improves path planning and room coverage.
17. Multi-floor maps are important for duplex or multi-story homes.
18. No-go zones and no-mop zones should be supported in the app.
19. Map editing should allow room splitting, merging, and custom zones.
20. Obstacle detection should identify cords, shoes, toys, and pet waste if possible.
21. Carpet detection should boost suction and lift mop pads automatically.
22. Cliff sensors are required near stairs.
23. Edge sensors improve wall-following and corner cleaning.
24. App map visualization helps users find missed areas.
25. Firmware OTA support improves long-term feature and navigation updates.

## Cleaning Hardware
26. Anti-tangle brushes are important for long hair and pets.
27. Rubber brushes are easier to clean than dense bristle brushes.
28. Dual side brushes can improve corner pickup, but design quality matters.
29. HEPA H13-level filtration is helpful for allergies.
30. Dustbin capacity of 500 ml or more reduces emptying frequency.
31. Pet homes may prefer 600 ml dustbins or auto-empty stations.
32. Adjustable suction lets users balance noise and power.
33. Brush chamber design should be easy to remove and clean.
34. Water output should have at least three levels.
35. Electric rotating mops are better for sticky stains.
36. Vibrating mop systems can help with tile grout and light stains.
37. Sealed water tanks reduce leak risk.
38. Dirty-water tanks should be easy to remove and rinse.
39. Floating brushes improve contact across uneven surfaces.
40. Replaceable consumables should be affordable and easy to find.

## Smart Features
41. Basic app features should include scheduling, room cleaning, zones, no-go areas, and cleaning history.
42. Voice control is useful for hands-free use.
43. Multi-account sharing helps families manage the same robot.
44. Remote start is useful while traveling.
45. Cleaning logs should show area, duration, path, and status.
46. Consumable-life reminders help maintenance.
47. Error self-diagnosis reduces support friction.
48. Custom cleaning order helps large homes.
49. URL or cloud dependency should be considered if privacy matters.
50. Look for stable app reviews, not just hardware specs.

## Home Fit
51. Hardwood: prioritize low water output, mop lift, soft pads, and no-mop zones.
52. Tile: prioritize suction plus rotating or vibrating mop pads.
53. Carpet: prioritize suction, carpet boost, anti-tangle brush, and mop lift.
54. Pet homes: prioritize anti-tangle brush, large dustbin, auto-empty, and high-efficiency filter.
55. Children: prioritize obstacle avoidance, no-go zones, and safety locks.
56. Elderly users: prioritize simple app flow, physical buttons, and voice control.
57. Small apartments: prioritize compact size, quiet operation, and simple scheduling.
58. Large homes: prioritize runtime, big tanks, multi-map support, and resume cleaning.
59. High-dust homes: prioritize sealed dustbin, strong filter, and easy filter replacement.
60. Humid areas: prioritize mop drying, removable tanks, and easy cleaning station maintenance.

## Reliability And Ownership Cost
61. Check warranty length for the whole machine, motor, and battery.
62. A 2-year whole-machine warranty is stronger than a 1-year warranty.
63. Battery and motor warranty matter because repairs can be expensive.
64. Check side brush, main brush, filter, mop pad, dust bag, and battery costs before buying.
65. Confirm accessories are available locally.
66. Avoid non-original parts for seals, brushes, charging contacts, and mop modules.
67. Read long-term reviews about mapping, docking, battery, and service.
68. Buy from official channels to avoid refurbished or unsupported units.
69. Check serial number and manufacturing date when possible.
70. Inspect package, dock, power cable, filters, mop pads, and spare parts at unboxing.

## Practical Specs
71. Working noise under 55 dB is better for bedrooms and home offices.
72. Quiet mode usually lowers suction, so compare standard-mode noise too.
73. Lithium batteries are preferable to older nickel-cadmium batteries.
74. Battery capacity around 5200 mAh is useful for large homes.
75. Fast charging under 2 hours is convenient for frequent use.
76. Climbing ability around 2 cm is useful for thresholds.
77. Rubber drive wheels improve grip on carpet and wet tile.
78. IPX4 or better water resistance helps with damp floors, but the robot should never be soaked.
79. Strong cooling design protects motor life.
80. Charging dock should resist slipping and have protected contacts.

## Buying Timing And Value
81. Sales events can reduce price, but avoid choosing only by discount.
82. Compare real replacement-part cost over two years.
83. Premium stations are worth it if they save maintenance time you actually dislike.
84. For tight budgets, prioritize navigation, suction, and filtration over luxury station features.
85. For pet homes, auto-empty may be worth more than advanced mopping.
86. For hardwood homes, mop control may be worth more than maximum suction.
87. For large homes, runtime and resume cleaning matter more than peak suction alone.
88. For rental homes, portability and simple setup are more valuable.
89. For smart-home users, check compatibility with existing assistants.
90. For privacy-sensitive users, check camera usage and cloud requirements.

## Red Flags
91. No clear warranty or local service.
92. Consumables are expensive or unavailable.
93. App reviews mention frequent disconnects.
94. Mapping is random-only for a complex home.
95. No no-go zones or map editing.
96. Water output cannot be adjusted.
97. Mop pads are hard to wash or replace.
98. Dock requires too much manual cleaning for your lifestyle.
99. Battery replacement is difficult or unsupported.
100. Marketing emphasizes peak suction but ignores real cleaning tests, maintenance, and support.
